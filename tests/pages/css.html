What works today

The cascade: the user-agent stylesheet, author rules from <style> and a style= attribute, resolved by origin, specificity and source order, with !important, inheritance, calc(), custom properties and media queries.

Boxes: colours, fonts, margins — including auto margins, which is how a box is centred — padding, borders per side, widths and heights, and text-align. On an inline box too: its padding and borders take room in the line, and a box broken over two lines keeps the border on the edge each piece owns.

Shadows: box-shadow with an offset, a blur and a spread, several at once, painted behind the box in the order CSS gives them.

Gradients: a linear-gradient background, by angle or by keyword, with stops that are spread evenly when they say nothing.

Grid: display: grid with grid-template-columns and grid-template-rows in lengths, percentages, fr, auto and repeat() of a definite count or of auto-fill, a gap between the tracks, and items placed in order — or on the line grid-column names, over as many tracks as it says to span, with the rest placed around them and the cells they stepped over left free.

Rounded corners: border-radius per corner, on the background and on a border that is the same on all four sides. A radius larger than the box is scaled down rather than folding over itself.

Overflow: hidden, scroll and auto all cut their contents off at the padding edge and establish a formatting context, and a box with more in it than it can show scrolls under the wheel, with a scrollbar to say where you are.

Position: relative moves a box without moving its neighbours, absolute takes it out of the flow and places it against the nearest positioned ancestor, fixed places it against the viewport and leaves it there while the page scrolls, and sticky holds a box at its inset until its container runs out from under it. A positioned box paints over what it overlaps, and z-index moves it further up or below the flow.

Flex: display: flex with a direction, a gap, flex-grow and flex-shrink, justify-content, align-items and align-self, and wrapping onto as many lines as the items need. An item with no width of its own is as wide as its content wants to be.

Floats: float and clear, with the lines beside a float shortened one at a time. Stylesheets from <link> are fetched, and so are the pictures an <img> asks for.

Selector matching, over our own DOM: type, class, id, attribute (including ^=, $=, *=), the four combinators, structural pseudo-classes, and :is(), :where() and :not(). The --dump-selectors flag answers "which elements does this selector match" against a real document.

What does not work yet

A rule the cascade computes correctly can still change nothing, because layout reads a subset of what CSS defines: an unsupported display lays out as a block, a grid item cannot be given a *named* line and grid-auto-flow: dense is not read, and a stacking context is not a thing yet — `z-index` orders boxes against the whole page rather than against their nearest positioned ancestor. In a flex container, order, align-content and baseline alignment are not read, an item may shrink below its content, and inline-flex is placed as a block. A float with no width of its own takes the whole column rather than shrinking to its contents, and only a float establishes a formatting context of its own, so overflow does not yet stop one.

An inline box is as tall as the line it sits on rather than as tall as its own text, and vertical-align does not exist. Border styles other than solid are drawn solid, a border that is rounded and different on each side falls back to four square-cornered rectangles, and a rounded box clips its contents at a square edge. A resize that no rule reads costs nothing — the sheets are parsed once and the cascade is skipped — but a resize that does change something recomputes every element rather than the part that changed.
